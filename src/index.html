GRAPHIC IMAGE FOR THE

SANT JOAN 2018 AWARDS

Challenge

Briefing: realization of the graphic proposal for the thirty-eighth edition of the Sant Joan Awards.

Concept: exclusivity of the Catalan language.

Catalan is a language that has its own letters and spellings, which allows fast differentiation and recognition. Therefore, my proposal seeks to unite the ideas of Literature, referring to the theme of the contest, and Catalan, to honor the language used in it.

Solution

My proposal is an alphabet apparently normal that hides something that the viewer must discover. It is a tribute to the Catalan language and a claim to its use in literature.

The reason why the letters are in lowercase is that, in prose, the capital letter is only used width proper names or at the beginning of a sentence. The rest must be in lowercase. I am not presenting independent letters among them but spellings that, combined, form words and communicate.

I have also worked with an optical effect that, through color, differentiates the spellings of a common alphabet from those of the Catalan language. This gives the viewer a clue so that he knows that the proposal hides something he must discover himself. The intention is not to reveal the trick but to show that there is magic in it. The tones used do not break with the graphic of the contest sponsors and, therefore, it does not seem like an independent proposal.

I think it is important to emphasize the importance of the Catalan language and encourage its use in literature. That's why I wanted to pay tribute to him with this proposal.

CoConcept has done a great job combining the two areas most present in the contest. For us, it is important to promote the Catalan literature and this proposal is a direct claim to its use.

Challenge

Underground parking that consists of a platform that is activated when placed on top with the car and an elevator and conveyor belts that park it and return it to you. SMART PERSONAL

MOBILE WORKSPACE VR

Challenge

A selected group of students at ESDi has worked on the design of an adapted Segway-like vehicle that would allow humans to travel around interior spaces in Mars carrying an HP's HTC Vive backpack station with a VR workstation environment that students have also designed to allow humans to work remotely in Mars.

The red planet presents extremely different climatic and environmental conditions than planet Earth. In order to be able to save resources and psace in Mars, humans will have to work remotely using a virtualized workspace inspired by current working places on Earth.
The adapted Segway vehicle has been designed with a special case that will allow users to stop the vehicle in any indoor space, seat comfortably and use the VR workstation. Two integrated sensors will allow the VR headset to geo-localize the users so that they can move within the virtual space. The vehicle design also integrates storage space and lighting. We imagine that these adapted Segways could be available in any indoor space in Mars for people to take, use and drop off at designated spots, a sort of Citybike-Office in Mars.

Solution

Moreover, the virtualization of the traditional office space offered an oppportunity for students to re- think current workspaces and to design the tools and features that the working spaces of the future should have considering the opportunities that VR has to offer.

The team of students and professors at ESDi Design School conducted research on how people interact with physical elements in their working environments as well as on how they interact with coworkers and clients at the office. That information was used to design the virtual working environment presented in this proposal, which includes improvements over current (real) office spaces such as: speech to text translation, 360º video meetings, 360º canvases, 3D skething, smart calendar and to-does apps, and a three-dimension re-design of the traditional computer interface.

The proposed virtual workspace represents a starting point, we look forward to developing it further in the following development phase. For example, in its final form, the VR workspace would be completely customizable by users. They would be able to choose different materials to decorate their virtual offices, they could design or buy objects of their choice or pick the view that thy would like to have from their VR office window.

As a starting point, we have designed a neutral room based in the concept of "harborage". Assuming that in the barren landscape of Mars humans will miss the comfort of home, students built the working environment with warm materials such as wood and velvet. Likewise, the use of oval shapes in the design of the objects that inhabit the VR workspace aim to recall the idea of planet, orbit, and cosmos and remind us that the user is at the center of the environment and tools and space are around it.

Besides designing the virtual office space, students have worked from the user experience point of view designing the interaction with the objects in the virtual workspace of the future in Mars. There has been a re-design of current common office tools at three different levels of relevance according to the activities that the user does within the environment.

At level one, the first thing users will see when they put the VR goggles on, will be a floating surrounding mesh-looking desk with apps on it: a to does list, an agenda, a communication device, a browser, and a tool box.
At level two, to the user's right, there will be a bookshelf, which will be a library containing custom visual and written media that users will have requested quick access to.
At a third level, they will find lighting and settings' control knobs as well as the trashcan, in the ceiling and in the floor respectively.

Users will be able to move apps around and, at any given point, they will be able to make apps fill the 360º screen. That is, if users need to play a video from the library, they will be able to play it in full screen mode, maximizing the visualization capabilities of HTC Vive.

Users will also be able to see, as they get into their VR workspace, who else is online and communicate with their coworkers using the communication device, which will include text, voice, email, camera and a virtual conference room.

Last but not least, the proposed VR workspace will have a time-machine feature so that users will be able to travel to see past and future states of their office. For example, if they remember they left a to-do note on their VR "desks" a week ago and they cannot fin it, they will be able to travel back to see the state in which the office was left that day.

The interaction with all these applications and with the space will be through carefully thought gestures and voice inputs, which students already designed and are to be developed in the next phase of the project. The current proposal includes renderings and a first basic 3D modeling of both the adapted-for-working-anywhere vehicle and the VR workstation environment. We very look forward to adding interaction elements and full functinality to these prototypes in the following development phase.

The team has worked with great involvement and commitment, resulting in a virtual experience that responds to the needs of future users working on Mars.
